# Laser Hair Removal for Dark Skin in Amman: What You Need to Know in 2026

One of the most common questions we receive at ArtiZone is some version of this: *"I have dark skin — is laser hair removal safe for me?"*

The honest answer is: **yes, absolutely — but only with the right laser.** The wrong laser on darker skin can cause burns, blistering, and post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH). The right laser, used correctly, is just as safe and effective on dark skin as it is on fair skin.

This matters enormously in Amman and Jordan, where olive, medium-brown, and darker skin tones are the norm — not the exception. If you have been told laser hair removal is not suitable for your skin tone, or if you have had a bad experience elsewhere, this guide explains exactly what you need to know.

---

## Why Skin Tone Matters for Laser Hair Removal

To understand why skin tone matters, you need to understand how laser hair removal works.

Laser hair removal works by targeting **melanin** — the pigment that gives hair its colour. The laser energy is absorbed by melanin in the hair follicle, converts to heat, and destroys the follicle's ability to produce new hair.

The problem is that melanin is also present in the skin itself. In darker skin tones, there is more melanin in the skin — which means certain lasers can be absorbed by the skin as well as the hair, causing:

- **Burns and blistering** at the skin surface
- **Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH)** — dark patches that can persist for months
- **Hypopigmentation** — light patches where melanin has been destroyed
- **Scarring** in severe cases

These risks are not inherent to laser hair removal — they are specific to using the wrong laser type or incorrect settings for darker skin.

## The Fitzpatrick Scale: Understanding Your Skin Type

Dermatologists classify skin tones using the **Fitzpatrick Scale**, which runs from Type I (very fair, always burns) to Type VI (very dark, never burns).

Most Jordanian and Arab skin tones fall in the **Type III–V** range:

| Fitzpatrick Type | Description | Common in |
|---|---|---|
| Type I | Very fair, always burns, never tans | Northern European |
| Type II | Fair, usually burns, sometimes tans | Northern European |
| Type III | Medium, sometimes burns, always tans | Mediterranean, Middle Eastern |
| Type IV | Olive, rarely burns, always tans | Arab, South Asian, Hispanic |
| Type V | Brown, very rarely burns | South Asian, Middle Eastern, African |
| Type VI | Very dark, never burns | African, South Asian |

Types III–V are the most common in Jordan — and these are precisely the skin tones that require careful laser selection.

## Which Laser Is Safe for Dark Skin?

Not all lasers are equal when it comes to darker skin tones. Here is how the main types compare:

### Nd:YAG 1064nm — The Gold Standard for Dark Skin ✓

The **Nd:YAG laser** (neodymium-doped yttrium aluminium garnet, 1064nm wavelength) is the safest and most effective laser for darker skin tones. Here is why:

The 1064nm wavelength is **less absorbed by melanin in the skin** than shorter wavelengths. This means the laser energy passes through the skin surface with minimal absorption and targets the deeper hair follicle directly — without heating the surrounding skin.

The result: effective hair removal with a dramatically lower risk of burns, PIH, or hypopigmentation on darker skin.

**Nd:YAG is safe for Fitzpatrick Types I–VI** — including the darkest skin tones.

### Diode 810nm — Safe for Types I–IV with Correct Settings

The diode laser (810nm) is highly effective and widely used. It is safe for Fitzpatrick Types I–IV (fair to olive skin) when used with correct settings and cooling. For Types V–VI, the risk of PIH increases and Nd:YAG is preferred.

### Alexandrite 755nm — Not Recommended for Dark Skin ✗

The alexandrite laser has a shorter wavelength that is more strongly absorbed by melanin. It is highly effective on fair skin (Types I–III) but carries a significant risk of burns and PIH on Types IV–VI. It should not be used on olive or darker skin.

### IPL (Intense Pulsed Light) — Not a Laser, Not Recommended for Dark Skin ✗

IPL is not a laser — it emits a broad spectrum of light rather than a single wavelength. This makes it less precise and more likely to be absorbed by skin melanin. IPL is not recommended for Types IV–VI and has a poor safety record on darker skin tones.

## Common Concerns for Darker Skin Clients

### "I had a bad experience with laser elsewhere — burns and dark marks."

This is unfortunately common — and almost always the result of using the wrong laser type or incorrect settings for your skin tone. Alexandrite lasers and IPL devices used on darker skin are the most frequent culprits.

At ArtiZone, we use Nd:YAG laser specifically because it is the safest option for the skin tones most common in Jordan. We also perform patch tests before every new client's first full session.

### "Will laser make my skin darker?"

With the correct laser and settings, no. PIH (post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation) is a risk with the wrong laser — not with Nd:YAG used correctly. If you experience any darkening after a session, we adjust settings immediately.

### "My skin is very sensitive — can I still do laser?"

Skin sensitivity and skin tone are separate concerns. Sensitive skin requires gentler settings and longer intervals between sessions — but it does not prevent laser hair removal. We assess sensitivity during your consultation and adjust the protocol accordingly.

### "I have PCOS / hormonal hair growth — will laser work?"

Laser hair removal reduces hair significantly but hormonal hair growth can cause some regrowth over time. Clients with PCOS typically need more sessions initially and 2–3 maintenance sessions per year. Laser still dramatically reduces the burden of hair removal compared to waxing or threading.

## Before and After Your Session: Skin Care for Darker Tones

### 2 weeks before:
- Avoid sun exposure and tanning — increased melanin in the skin raises the risk of PIH
- Stop waxing, threading, and epilating (shaving is fine — it leaves the root intact for the laser to target)
- Avoid retinol and AHA/BHA products for 1 week before

### Day of treatment:
- Shave the area 12–24 hours before (not on the day)
- Come with clean, product-free skin
- No fake tan or self-tanner

### After treatment:
- Apply SPF 50+ every morning without exception — this is the single most important step to prevent PIH
- Avoid sun exposure for 48 hours
- No heat: saunas, steam rooms, hot baths for 24–48 hours
- No exfoliation for 5–7 days
- Moisturise daily — hydrated skin heals faster

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Nd:YAG laser as effective as other lasers?**

Yes — for darker skin tones, Nd:YAG is not just the safest option, it is also the most effective. The 1064nm wavelength penetrates deeper into the follicle, which can actually produce better results on coarser, darker hair than shorter-wavelength lasers.

**How many sessions do I need?**

Most clients need 6–8 sessions for significant permanent reduction. Hormonal areas (face, bikini) typically need more. Sessions are spaced 4–6 weeks apart for body areas and 3–4 weeks for facial areas.

**Can I do laser during summer?**

Yes — with precautions. Avoid treating recently tanned skin and maintain strict SPF 50+ use. We may adjust settings slightly during summer months for clients with sun-exposed skin.

**What areas can be treated?**

All body areas: legs, underarms, bikini/Brazilian, arms, back, chest, stomach, face (upper lip, chin, sideburns, neck), and more.

**Is it safe during pregnancy?**

Laser hair removal is not recommended during pregnancy. We advise waiting until after delivery and, if breastfeeding, until breastfeeding has stopped.

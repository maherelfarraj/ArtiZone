# Acne Solutions: Why You Still Break Out — and How to Finally Clear Your Skin

Acne is the most common skin condition in the world — and one of the most frustrating. You've tried the cleansers, the spot treatments, the expensive serums. You've cut out dairy, reduced sugar, and drunk more water. And yet the breakouts keep coming.

The reason most acne treatments fail is simple: they treat the symptom, not the cause. This guide explains what's actually driving your breakouts — and what genuinely works to stop them.

## What Actually Causes Acne?

Acne isn't caused by dirty skin. It's a complex condition involving four key factors that interact with each other:

### 1. Excess Sebum Production
Sebaceous glands produce sebum (skin oil) to lubricate and protect the skin. When these glands overproduce — often triggered by hormones, stress, or genetics — excess oil mixes with dead skin cells and clogs pores.

### 2. Clogged Pores (Comedones)
When dead skin cells don't shed properly, they combine with sebum to form a plug inside the pore. This creates blackheads (open comedones) and whiteheads (closed comedones) — the foundation of all acne.

### 3. Bacterial Overgrowth
*Cutibacterium acnes* (formerly *P. acnes*) is a bacteria that lives naturally on skin. When pores become clogged, this bacteria multiplies rapidly in the oxygen-free environment, triggering inflammation.

### 4. Inflammation
The immune system responds to bacterial overgrowth with inflammation — producing the red, swollen, painful papules and pustules we recognise as acne. In some cases, this inflammation goes deeper, forming nodules and cysts.

## Types of Acne — and Why It Matters

Different types of acne require different treatments. Using the wrong approach can make things worse.

| Type | Appearance | Best Treatment Approach |
|------|-----------|------------------------|
| Blackheads | Open, dark-tipped pores | Salicylic acid, professional extractions |
| Whiteheads | Closed, flesh-coloured bumps | Retinol, gentle exfoliation |
| Papules | Small, red, inflamed bumps | Niacinamide, benzoyl peroxide |

| Nodules | Large, hard, deep bumps | Prescription treatment, professional care |
| Cysts | Large, painful, fluid-filled | Dermatologist/specialist treatment |

## The Most Common Acne Triggers

### Hormonal Fluctuations
Androgens (male hormones present in both men and women) stimulate sebum production. Hormonal acne typically appears along the jawline, chin, and lower cheeks — and often flares around menstruation, during pregnancy, or at menopause.

### Stress
Stress triggers the release of cortisol, which increases sebum production and inflammation. This is why breakouts often appear before important events — your skin is responding to your stress hormones.

### Diet
The evidence on diet and acne is growing. High-glycaemic foods (white bread, sugar, processed carbohydrates) spike insulin levels, which in turn increase androgen activity and sebum production. Dairy — particularly skimmed milk — has also been linked to acne in some studies.

### Wrong Skincare Products
Using products that are too harsh strips the skin's natural barrier, triggering compensatory oil production. Using products that are too rich or comedogenic (pore-blocking) adds to congestion. Many "acne" products actually make skin worse over time.

### Over-Cleansing
Washing your face more than twice a day disrupts the skin microbiome and strips protective oils, leading to rebound oiliness and increased breakouts.

## What Actually Works: Evidence-Based Acne Solutions

### At Home

**Salicylic Acid (BHA)**
A beta-hydroxy acid that penetrates into pores and dissolves the sebum and dead skin cells that cause blockages. Use in a cleanser or toner 1–2 times daily. Ideal for blackheads, whiteheads, and mild inflammatory acne.

**Benzoyl Peroxide**
Kills acne-causing bacteria and reduces inflammation. Highly effective for inflammatory acne (papules and pustules). Start with 2.5% to minimise irritation. Use as a spot treatment or leave-on gel.

**Retinol / Retinoids**
Accelerates cell turnover, preventing dead skin cells from clogging pores. Also reduces post-acne marks over time. Start slowly (2–3 nights per week) and always use SPF during the day.

**Niacinamide**
Reduces sebum production, calms inflammation, and fades post-acne hyperpigmentation. One of the most versatile and well-tolerated acne ingredients. Use as a serum morning and evening.

**Azelaic Acid**
Antibacterial, anti-inflammatory, and brightening. Particularly effective for hormonal acne and post-acne marks. Suitable for sensitive skin.

## Treating Post-Acne Marks

Clearing active acne is only half the battle. Post-acne marks — the red, pink, or brown discolouration left after a breakout heals — can persist for months or years without treatment.

**Post-inflammatory erythema (PIE):** Red or pink marks. Respond well to niacinamide, azelaic acid, and LED therapy.

**Post-inflammatory hyperpigmentation (PIH):** Brown or dark marks, more common in darker skin tones. Respond to Vitamin C, kojic acid, azelaic acid, and chemical peels.

**Acne scars (textural):** Depressed or raised scars. Require professional treatment — microneedling, chemical peels, or RF microneedling for best results.

## Building an Acne-Clearing Routine

### Morning
1. Gentle gel cleanser (salicylic acid if oily/acne-prone)
2. Niacinamide serum
3. Oil-free, non-comedogenic moisturiser
4. **SPF 50** (essential — UV exposure worsens post-acne marks)

### Evening
1. Double cleanse (oil cleanser first if wearing SPF/makeup, then gel cleanser)
2. Salicylic acid toner or BHA exfoliant (3–4 nights per week)
3. Retinol (alternate nights with BHA, build up slowly)
4. Light moisturiser

### What to Avoid
- Picking or squeezing spots (spreads bacteria, causes scarring)
- Harsh physical scrubs (micro-tears worsen inflammation)
- Alcohol-based toners (strip barrier, trigger rebound oiliness)
- Heavy, occlusive creams (trap sebum in pores)
- Skipping SPF (UV exposure darkens post-acne marks significantly)
